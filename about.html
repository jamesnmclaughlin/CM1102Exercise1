About

Born 1906, Grace Hopper was an American mathematician, as well as a rear admiral in the US Navy who helped to devise the first commercial electronic computer called the UNIVAC I. She also devised naval applications for common-business-orientated language (COBOL).

She believed that programs should be written in a language that was fairly close to our English language, compared to machine code. This took a while to be accepted due to people believing that computers could not understand English.

She pioneered the implementation of standards for testing computer systems and components. These were for languages such as FORTRAN and COBOL. Because the navy tested conformance to these programming languages, it led to significant convergence of many programming language dialects of major computer vendors.

The tests that took place were assumed by the National Bureau of Standards and is know today as the National Institute of Standards and Technology.

Timeline

1928

Graduated from Vassar College after studying maths and physics.

1934

She decided to study at Yale University, where she acieved a masters and PhD in mathematics. She became one of the first few women to achieve this type of degree. She went back to Vassar College to teach mathematics.

1943

Leaving teaching, she decided to join the Naval Reserve, serving in WAVES (Women Accepted for Voluntary Emergency Service)

1944

During her time at the Naval Reserve, she became a lieutenant and was assigned at Harvard University to the Bureau of Ordnance’s Computation Project. At Harvard, she worked on the first large-scale automatic calculator called the Mark I. It was counted as a precursor of electronic computers. The Mark I was also known as the Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator.

1949

She joims the Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation

1951

The corporation is taken over by Remington Rand

1952

She designs an improved compiler. This improved compiler translated a programmer’s instructions to computer codes. The compiler was a precursor for COBOL which was a widely adapted language used around the world. As well as this, she oversaw the programming for the first commercial electronic computer, UNIVAC.

1955

Sperry Rand Corporation takes over from Remington Rand. Her division developes the first English-language data-processing compiler called the Flow-Matic.

1962

Elected a fellow of the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers

1966-67

Retired from the navy as command but was recalled a year later to help teach and standardise the navy's computer languages.

1967

Began serving as the Director of the Navy Programming Languages Group. She advocated for the Defense department to replace large, centralised systems with networks of small distributed computers. This meant that any user on any computer node could access common databases located on the network.

1969

Named first computer science Man of the Year by DPMA (Data Processing Management Assosciation)

1973

Promoted to the rank of Captain

1986

Retires from US naval duty at the age of 79, making her the oldest office on active duty.

1991

Awarded the National Medal of Technology

1992

Died on January 1, 1992 at the age of 85

2016

Awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom
